Set in Edinburgh

With its winding old town streets, gothic spires piercing the gray skies, and historic significance, Edinburgh has enthralled writers for centuries and become the setting of countless stories. Here are some of our favourite books set in the fair Scottish capital.

Highlight: The Fair Botanists

By Sara Sheridan

Story taster:

Could one rare plant hold the key to a thousand riches? It's the summer of 1822 and Edinburgh's botanical circles are gripped with excitement. In the newly-installed Botanic Garden, the Agave Americana plant looks set to flower - an event which only occurs once in several decades.

Newly widowed Elizabeth, just arrived in Edinburgh to put her unhappy past in London behind her, and Belle Brodie, a vivacious young woman with a passion for botany and the lucrative, dark art of perfume creation, meet over their shared excitement for the Garden. Both carry secrets. But as they are about to discover, secrets don't last long in this Enlightenment city. And when they are revealed, they can carry the greatest of consequences...

The Fair Botanists was selected as the Waterstones Scottish Book of the Year 2022. It's a beautiful, immersive read and a gem in the treasure trove of Edinburgh-based fiction.

"Joyously seductive prose with evocatively-drawn characters, The Fair Botanists is a beautiful tale of scandal and intrigue firmly rooted in the capital of 1820s Scotland."
- Susan Stokes-Chapman

Edinburgh Authors

Many authors have taken inspiration from this city and let its charm seep into their writing and lives. Some were born here, some arrived later in life, but all of the following authors have chosen to make Edinburgh their home. Below are some of our recommendations from the Edinburgh literary scene.

Highlight: Hamnet

By Maggie O'Farrell

Story taster:

On a summer's day in 1596, a young girl in Stratford-upon-Avon takes to her bed with a fever. Her twin brother, Hamnet, searches everywhere for help. Why is nobody at home?

Their mother, Agnes, is over a mile away, in the garden where she grows medicinal herbs. Their father is working in London. Neither parent knows that one of the children will not survive the week.

Hamnet is a tender and unforgettable reimagining of a boy whose life has been all but forgotten, but whose name was given to one of the most celebrated plays ever written. It is the winner of the Women's Prize for Fiction 2020.

"The story of Hamnet Shakespeare has been waiting in the shadows for over four hundred years. Maggie O'Farrell brings it dazzlingly, devastatingly, into the light."
- Kamila Shamsie

Scottish Literature

Edinburgh isn't the only city in Scotland that has produced high-caliber writers and writings. Authors from across the country have long produced notable works that have had a lasting effect on readers and global literary culture. Here are some of our picks of the best books from across the country.

Highlight: Young Mungo

By Douglas Stuart

Story taster:

Born under different stars, Protestant Mungo and Catholic James live in the hyper-masculine and violently sectarian world of Glasgow's housing estates. They should be sworn enemies if they're to be seen as men at all. As they find themselves falling in love, they dream of escaping the grey city, and Mungo works especially hard to hide his true self from all those around him.

But the threat of discovery is constant and the punishment unspeakable. When Mungo's mother sends him on a fishing trip to a loch in Western Scotland with two strange men whose drunken banter belies murky pasts, he will need to summon all his inner strength and courage to get back to a place of safety, a place where he and James might still have a future.

From the Booker-prizewinning author of Shuggie Bain, Young Mungo is a gripping and revealing story about the bounds of masculinity, the push and pull of family, the violence faced by so many queer people, and the dangers of loving someone too much. It has been shortlisted for the British Book Awards 2023 Fiction Book of the Year.

"I wasn't sure Young Mungo could live up to Shuggie Bain, but it surpasses it. Deeply harrowing but gently infused with hope & love. And so exquisitely written. It's a joy to watch, in real time, as Douglas Stuart takes his place as one of the greats of Scottish literature."
- Nicola Sturgeon
